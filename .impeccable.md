## Design Context

### Users
This project primarily serves a single administrator operating a graduation-project MVP attendance system in a desktop or laptop browser. Their main jobs are to manage employees and devices, configure attendance rules, oversee face-enrollment tasks, review attendance history, and provision devices during demo or lab setup. The highest-friction moments are initial device setup, enrollment status tracking, and explaining system status quickly during a demo or defense.

### Brand Personality
Treat Hitomi as industrial, precise, and uncompromising. The interface should feel like a control console tied to real hardware, not a soft SaaS admin panel. Keep Chinese-first copy direct and operational, and make the product feel sturdy, technical, and ready for device setup, diagnostics, and supervision.

### Aesthetic Direction
Bias the product toward an industrial hardware-console aesthetic: dense surfaces, squared edges, strong seams, muted steel neutrals, and controlled safety-color accents. Light and dark mode can both exist, but neither should feel airy or decorative. Prefer panel-like composition, hard borders, grid structure, and instrument-style labels over rounded cards and translucent layers. Avoid glassmorphism, oversized radii, soft wellness-dashboard gradients, and anything that looks like a generic startup admin template.

### Design Principles
1. Hardware first: every major surface should read like equipment software, not a consumer web app.
2. Hard edges over softness: use compact radii, solid fills, visible borders, and clear separation between panels.
3. Operational clarity beats decoration: metrics, statuses, timestamps, and device identifiers must be immediately scannable.
4. Setup flows must reduce uncertainty: serial configuration and enrollment tooling should surface the next action and recovery path clearly.
5. Demo credibility matters: the interface should help explain that this system controls real devices and real operational state.
